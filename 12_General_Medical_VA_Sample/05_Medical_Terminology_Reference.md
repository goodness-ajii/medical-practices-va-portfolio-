## Purpose

A working knowledge of medical terminology is what separates a healthcare-aware VA from a generic admin VA. This reference card is **what I keep open during every shift** to communicate fluently with providers, document accurately, and respond intelligently to patients.

It covers the **most common 100+ terms** a general medical VA encounters.

> As a Registered Nurse, I'm fluent in this terminology already — this card exists to help any practice I work with see exactly what I bring on day one.

## 7. Common Documentation Phrases

These are phrases I use and recognize daily in chart notes:

| Phrase | What It Means |
|---|---|
| "Patient denies fever, chills, or headache" | None of those symptoms reported |
| "Vitals stable, no acute distress" | Patient looks well, vitals normal |
| "Reviewed risks/benefits, patient consents" | Provider got informed consent |
| "Continue current management" | No changes to treatment |
| "F/u in 3 months for repeat labs" | Come back in 3 months for blood work |
| "Refer to [specialist] for further evaluation" | Send to a specialist |
| "Counseled on lifestyle modification" | Talked about diet, exercise, weight, etc. |
| "Pt verbalizes understanding" | Patient confirmed they understood |
| "WNL" | Within normal limits |
| "Negative for…" | Test or symptom is absent |
| "Positive for…" | Test or symptom is present |
| "Resolved" | Issue cleared up |
| "Stable" | Condition unchanged |
| "Worsening" | Condition declining |

## 10. Phrases I Translate for Patient Communication

These are clinical shorthand that should **never** appear in an email or message to a patient:

| Never to a patient | Translate to plain English |
|---|---|
| "Your A1C is 7.2 — DM2 not at goal" | "Your blood sugar marker came back slightly higher than your provider wants. They'd like to discuss it at your next visit." |
| "Negative ROS, WNL, f/u 3mo" | "Everything looks normal — please come back in 3 months for a follow-up." |
| "PMH significant for HTN, HLD" | "Your past medical history includes high blood pressure and high cholesterol." |
| "Rx renewed x 90d" | "Your prescription has been renewed for 90 days." |

This is a critical VA skill: **bridging clinical language and patient-friendly language without losing accuracy.**

---

## 11. Why This Matters for a VA

When a provider says **"send a 48-hour f/u email to Mr. Anderson re: his A1C — he's PRN insulin now and we want to recheck FBS in 6 weeks"** — a VA needs to know:

- f/u = follow-up
- A1C = his diabetes marker
- PRN insulin = "as-needed" insulin (he's now using it)
- FBS = fasting blood sugar
- Recheck in 6 weeks = schedule a labs visit at that interval

**That single sentence translates into 4 admin tasks.** A VA who recognizes the terms knocks them all out in 5 minutes. A VA who doesn't asks the provider to repeat themselves and creates friction.

That's the difference between **a fluent healthcare VA and an admin VA in scrubs.**
